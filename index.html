Introducing Creativity

Creativity is at the heart of early childhood education. It allows children to explore, imagine, experiment, and express themselves in different ways. Creativity is not only about making art or music; it is about how children think, solve problems, and try new ideas. In early learning, creativity happens every day when children play, build, draw, dance, or act out stories. It is through these activities that they make sense of their world and communicate their ideas (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

The Curriculum of Art

Importance of Art in Facilitating Creativity

Art plays a vital role in young children’s creativity by offering open-ended opportunities for exploration, expression, and communication. It helps children represent their ideas and feelings, test possibilities, and solve problems using various media (Boyd & Cutcher, 2015). Engaging in artistic processes promotes imagination, self-confidence, and perseverance as children experiment with materials, colours, and forms. As Cutcher and Boyd (2016) highlight, art encourages collaboration and dialogue, helping children learn from and with others. Through both individual and group art experiences, children build a strong sense of identity and belonging (EYLF Outcomes 1 and 2).

Creativity Theories and Perspectives

Art education in early childhood is strongly influenced by constructivist theories and sociocultural perspectives. Vygotsky’s theory of social learning emphasises the importance of interaction, collaboration, and guided participation in creative activities. The Reggio Emilia approach views art as one of the “hundred languages of children,” encouraging multiple forms of expression and meaning-making (Cutcher & Boyd, 2016). Gardner’s theory of multiple intelligences highlights the importance of visual-spatial intelligence, acknowledging children’s capacity to think and communicate through images and design (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). These theories encourage teachers to provide environments and materials that foster curiosity, experimentation, and collaboration.

Resources, Materials, and Digital Technologies

To promote creativity in art, teachers can offer a variety of open-ended resources, including paints, pastels, clay, natural materials, fabric scraps, and recycled items (Boyd & Cutcher, 2015). Materials should invite exploration and experimentation rather than prescribe specific outcomes. Teachers can also integrate digital technologies like drawing apps, photography, and digital storytelling tools, enabling children to extend their creative expressions into multimedia formats (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). The key is to balance traditional and digital media, ensuring that all tools support children’s agency and creative intentions rather than limiting their possibilities.

The Curriculum of Drama & Puppetry

Importance in Facilitating Creativity

Drama and puppetry support young children’s creativity by giving them fun, safe ways to express their thoughts, ideas, and feelings. These activities help children act out real or imagined experiences, explore new ideas, and learn to solve problems. Drama and puppetry build confidence, communication skills, and help children understand their world (Tombak, 2014; Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Children work together, share ideas, and take on different roles. This helps them learn social skills and develop empathy. Drama and puppetry also support language growth as children plan, act out, and talk about their play (ETCH107, 2024).

Creativity Theories and Perspectives

Drama and puppetry link to Vygotsky’s theory, which highlights that children learn best through social interaction. When children act out ideas or use puppets together, they practise sharing, turn-taking, and problem-solving. The Reggio Emilia approach encourages children to express their ideas in many ways, including through role-play and puppetry — known as the “hundred languages of children” (Anderberg & Kratochvil, 2005). Creative dramatics is focused on the process, helping children build skills to explore, communicate, and understand their world (Anderberg & Kratochvil, 2005). Gardner’s multiple intelligences theory shows drama helps children who learn through movement and social interaction (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

Resources, Materials, and Digital Technologies

Teachers can support drama and puppetry by offering dress-ups, hats, scarves, simple props (like boxes or chairs), and a range of puppets (sock, stick, finger, or hand puppets). These open-ended resources encourage children to create their own stories and characters (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Teachers can also use storybooks to inspire role-play (Wanerman, 2010). Digital technologies, such as audio recorders or storytelling apps, can help children create and share their own stories, record puppet shows, or explore voices and sounds for characters (Anders, 2021). The key is to provide tools that allow children to lead their play and ideas.

EYLF 2: Children are connected with and contribute to their world through collaborative play.

Age Group: 3–5 years

Children pretend to use cups in pretend tea party, café, or shop play. Children assume server or customer roles and have conversations and social interactions (Wanerman, 2010).

EYLF 2: Children use role-play to build relationships and connections.

The Curriculum of Movement & Music

Importance in Facilitating Creativity

Movement and music are creative and imaginative ways through which children learn and share. By music-making, singing, and movement to music, children learn their body, feelings, and thoughts in new and imaginative ways (Niland, 2015). Movement to music helps them acquire balance, coordination, and confidence. Singing and music-making help them listen, remember, and express affect. Exercises like dancing or making rhythms encourage children in trying out new movements, changing their actions, and solving problems (Susanne, 2008). Music and movement in early childhood settings also build friendship and cooperation because children share space, move together, and enjoy creating together.

Creativity Theories and Perspectives

Movement and music are supported by learning theories focused on active play and social learning (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Vygotsky's socio-cultural theory explains children learn through working together on activities, such as singing or dancing together. Reggio Emilia looks at music and movement as "languages" children can use to convey ideas and feelings (Dinham & Chalk, 2018). Gardner’s theory of multiple intelligences illustrates the ways in which music and movement aid children who can learn most easily using sound, rhythm, and movement. These ideas encourage educators to give children room to play with music and movement in flexible, individualized ways.

Resources, Materials, and Digital Technologies

Teachers can support music and movement by providing children with scarves, ribbons, drums, tambourines, shakers, and xylophones to explore (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Hearing music from diverse cultures and styles of music ignites imagination and movement. Hoops, mats, and soft balls facilitate active play. Digital tools, such as music-making software and dance videos, give children access to new tools for creating and exploring. Teachers can also project colorful pictures or backgrounds while doing activities to open up imagination. Taping performances and then listening to them again enables children to think about their movements and music-making, leading them to have ideas on how to create more or better work.

EYLF Outcome 5: Effective communicators.

EYLF Outcome 2: Connected with and contribute to their world.

The Curriculum of Language & Literacy

Importance in Facilitating Creativity

Language and literacy are similarly important in developing creativity because they help children explain concepts, tell stories, and express meaning in different forms. Creative language activities provide children with opportunities to play around with words and sounds and meaning, extending their imagination and confidence (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Literacy is more than reading and writing and also involves speaking, listening, singing, drawing, and role-playing. The EYLF (AGDE, 2022) states that children learn language and literacy best through real and play-based experiences. When teachers provide children with rich language experiences, they help children to become confident communicators who are able to think in imaginative ways and clearly articulate their thoughts.

Creativity Theories and Perspectives

Vygotsky's theory suggests that children learn language in social interaction and shared experience. In the Reggio Emilia approach, literacy is described as one of the "hundred languages of children," meaning that children are able to represent concepts in very imaginative forms, including words, movement, and art (AGDE, 2022). Gardner's theory of multiple intelligences names linguistic intelligence as a type of strength in the majority of children and encourages creative literacy activity such as rhymes, songs, and fantasy narrative (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). These theories remind teachers to develop interesting and interactive language activities that encourage children to explore, create, and convey meaningfully to them.

Resources, Materials, and Digital Technologies

Teachers can promote language and literacy creativity through offering various resources such as books, puppets, story cards, felt boards, and writing materials (Connor & Toper, 2015). Singing songs, reading rhymes, and chanting allow children to learn new words, listen, and learn rhythm in speech (Schiller, n.d.). Technology like interactive story applications and voice recorders offer children the chance to create and present their own stories (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Presenting bilingual books and multicultural props encourages children to honor and value other cultures and languages. The combination of traditional materials with digital technology allows teachers to create rich and innovative language learning experiences.

Language & Literacy Learning Experiences

Age Group: 0–2 years

Nursery Rhyme Actions

Children imitate and respond to rhymes like Twinkle, Twinkle Little Star using hand movements. Refines sense of rhythm, vocabulary, and memory (Schiller, n.d.).

EYLF Outcome 5: Children engage with a range of texts and make meaning from them.

A basket with objects from a simple picture book (e.g., Brown Bear, Brown Bear) lets infants explore items while hearing the story. Encourages word recognition and sensory exploration (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

EYLF Outcome 5: Children begin to understand how symbols and pattern systems work.

Age Group: 2–3 years

Children use animal puppets to retell a known story and add their own idea. Encourages expression and imaginative language (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

EYLF Outcome 5: Children are engaged in oral and non-verbal communication to express their thoughts.

With large books, kids trace images, predict what is going to happen, and repeat words. Builds vocabulary and comprehension (Connor & Toper, 2015).

EYLF Outcome 5: Children express ideas and make meaning from text.

Age Group: 3–5 years

Children work together to create a story while the teacher writes it down. They then make colorful caterpillar crafts using paper circles, glue, and decorations to illustrate the story. This activity supports creativity, sequencing, teamwork, and fine motor development (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

EYLF Outcome 5: Children create and share texts in different formats.

Children find objects that rhyme with a given word (e.g., “cat”—find” “hat”). Develops sound awareness and playful use of language (Schiller, n.d.).

EYLF Outcome 5: Children develop phonological awareness.

The Curriculum of Science

Importance in Facilitating Creativity

Science in early childhood is all about wondering, discovering how things happen, and investigating. Science turns children into problem-solvers through observing, predicting, experimenting, and asking questions. Science in play enables children to become curious and learn to think scientifically (Wilson, n.d.). Science makes children inventive because it makes them want to try out materials, inquire about ideas, and find out about the world around them. EYLF (AGDE, 2022) states that children must be provided with opportunities by educators to explore nature, technology, and common things. With this, children develop artistic and critical thinking skills that are the foundation of quality lifelong learning.

Creativity Theories and Perspectives

Vygotsky's sociocultural theory says children learn most effectively by sharing activities and interacting with others. Science activities such as group work or a nature walk allow children to share ideas and learn from each other. Reggio Emilia is also in support of science as inquiry-based learning with the children's questions leading the investigation. Gardner’s multiple intelligences theory highlights logical-mathematical and naturalist intelligences, which grow through activities like classifying, measuring, and experimenting (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). These theories show that science learning should be hands-on, collaborative, and guided by children’s interests, giving them freedom to explore, question, and create their own understanding of the world.

Resources, Materials, and Digital Technologies

Science is complemented with open-ended materials such as magnifying glasses, measuring cups, magnets, leaves, rocks, and water play materials (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Natural materials allow young children to observe closely, and recycled materials encourage building and testing. Digital technology such as tablets with nature software or kid-friendly microscopes can help children explore and document observations (NAEYC, n.d.). For preschool children, brief clips about animals or weather can be employed to generate interest and discussion. Combining hands-on materials and technology offers means for children to investigate science in imaginative, interactive, and developmentally appropriate ways to allow investigation and inventive problem-solving.

Science Learning Experiences

Age Group: 0–2 years

Water Play Exploration

Babies explore shallow trays of water with cups, spoons, and floating toys. They notice cause and effect and engage their senses (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

EYLF Outcome 4: Children develop curiosity and problem-solving skills.

Nature Texture Baskets

Baskets with leaves, pinecones, shells, and stones let infants explore textures and shapes while hearing simple descriptive words (Wilson, n.d.).

EYLF Outcome 5: Children connect language to sensory experiences.

Age Group: 2–3 years

Magnet Discovery

Children test magnets on different objects, predicting and checking results. Encourages early scientific thinking (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

EYLF Outcome 4: Children predict and test ideas.

Seed Planting

Children plant fast-growing seeds and care for them, observing changes over time (Wilson, n.d.).

EYLF Outcome 2: Children care for living things and observe growth.

Age Group: 3–5 years

Sink or Float Investigation

Children test objects in water, recording results with pictures or charts (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

EYLF Outcome 4: Children investigate properties through inquiry.

Shadow Play

Using a torch, children explore how shadows change when objects or lights move (Wilson, n.d.).

EYLF Outcome 5: Children describe and explore changes in their environment.

The Curriculum of Engineering

Importance in Facilitating Creativity

Early childhood engineering involves making things, designing, and problem-solving in order to accomplish an objective. It stimulates creativity since it allows children to generate ideas, plan, experiment, and reflect on what they have designed. Children learn from firsthand experiences about how materials behave and how to change them when they do not work (Zabawa, 2019). Engineering also helps children become persistent and confident by doing trial and error. EYLF (AGDE, 2022) encourages play and discovery where children can build, design, and experiment. In the process, children become capable problem solvers of real-world problems.

Creativity Theories and Perspectives

Vygotsky’s sociocultural theory says children learn best when they interact and work together. This makes group engineering projects useful for sharing ideas and solving problems together. The Reggio Emilia approach values hands-on learning and encourages children to design and build based on their own questions. Gardner’s multiple intelligences theory shows that spatial and logical-mathematical intelligences can grow through engineering challenges (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Piaget's constructivist theory also stresses active discovery learning. All the above perceptions point to the fact that engineering enables children to be creative by imagining, designing, experimenting, and improving on their own solutions and learning from others.

Resources, Materials, and Digital Technologies

Engineering activities can utilize items around the home such as blocks, boxes, tubes, tape, and string (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Loose materials such as bottle tops, bits of material, and sticks offer children open-ended instruments to plan and construct. Simple instruments such as child-safe scissors, rulers, and measuring tape promote planning and constructing. Technology, such as friendly design software, building games, or taking pictures, offers children the chance to take pictures and display projects (NAEYC, n.d.). Videos of actual engineering in the world, including bridge building or construction sites, can be employed in idea generation. The marriage of hands-on materials and technology supports creativity and problem-solving.

EYLF Outcome 4: Children experiment with fitting and matching.

EYLF Outcome 4: Children experiment with structures and materials through play.

Children use chunky foam or wooden shapes (triangles, squares, circles) to create simple buildings or patterns. This introduces basic shapes while encouraging creative design.

EYLF Outcome 4: Children use shapes and materials to represent ideas.

Age Group: 3–5 years

Children build bridges from blocks, straws, or sticks, testing strength with toy animals or cars (Zabawa, 2019).

EYLF Outcome 4: Children test and improve their designs.

Wind-Powered Vehicles

Children make small paper vehicles with sails and use a fan to move them, adjusting designs for better results (Major, 2018).

EYLF Outcome 4: Children solve design challenges creatively.

The Curriculum of Technologies

Importance in Facilitating Creativity

Technology in young children is not just screen use. Technology includes tools, materials, and processes that help young children to make, design, and repair things. When used thoughtfully, technology can encourage creativity by allowing children to try out ideas, try out solutions, and share work (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). This can include digital devices such as tablets and coding toys, and also non-digital products such as scissors, rulers, and construction sets. EYLF (AGDE, 2022) encourages technological play. Technology used in innovative ways results in children becoming confident problem-solvers and creative thinkers in readiness for learning in the future.

Creativity Theories and Perspectives

Vygotsky’s sociocultural theory explains that children learn best by interacting and working with others. This makes technology-rich group projects a good way to support creativity. The Reggio Emilia approach sees technology as one of the “hundred languages of children,” giving new ways to share ideas and record learning. Gardner’s multiple intelligences theory shows that spatial and logical skills can grow through activities that use technology and problem-solving (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Constructivist theory supports hands-on exploration, where children learn by doing. These perspectives show that technology helps children explore, create, and communicate while learning with and from others.

Resources, Materials, and Digital Technologies

Technology learning can use both digital and hands-on resources. Non-digital tools such as building kits, pulleys, measuring tapes, and art materials support problem-solving (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Tools such as tablets with drawing or storytelling software, coding devices, and child-friendly cameras give children new ways of creating and recording what they are doing (NAEYC, n.d.). For young children, this can be as simple as cause-and-effect toys, talking books, and music players. Real-life problem-solving videos, such as construction, can stimulate children's thoughts. Combining physical tools with technology gives children more choices for creative thinking and problem-solving.

The Curriculum of Mathematics & Numeracy

Importance in Facilitating Creativity

Math and numeracy in early childhood are about more than counting. They are about patterns, shapes, measuring, space, and problem solving in a context and an engaging way (Fleer & Raban, n.d.). Creative math’s activities stimulate children to think logically and use their imagination. These consist of block building, sorting, and generating patterns using colors or forms (McDonald, 2019). The EYLF (AGDE, 2022) assists teachers in integrating math’s into everyday play to make it fun and relevant to everyday life. By participating in imaginary play with math’s, children are able to try new things without fear, explore possibilities, and generate solutions—abilities that will assist them in learning now and in the future.

Loose materials such as pebbles, bottle tops, and shells can be used for counting, sorting, and making patterns. Material outside like sand, water, and chalk can be used for measurement and drawing shapes. Counting software, pattern games, and interactive whiteboards can facilitate enjoyable learning online (McDonald, 2019). Aged preschoolers can utilize simple coding tasks or computer math’s programs to solve problems. Combining physical and virtual materials offers children hands-on and visual opportunities for discovering math’s and creative thinking.

The Curriculum of Humanities and social sciences

Importance in Facilitating Creativity

Humanities and Social Sciences (HASS) in early childhood education includes history, geography, civics, and cultural studies. HASS allows children to learn about people, places, and the environment (ACARA, 2021). Creative HASS activities encourage children to explore their identity, respect other cultures, and value diverse cultures. Storytelling, role-playing, and mapping encourage curiosity, empathy, and problem-solving skills (Stonehouse, 2012). The Early Years Learning Framework (EYLF) (AGDE, 2022) enhances HASS learning as it fosters a sense of belonging, being, and becoming. As children learn creatively about the community and environment they live in, they develop the values and skills to engage actively and respectfully as members of society.

Creativity Theories and Perspectives

Vygotsky's sociocultural theory explains how children learn through social interaction and, thus, group discussion and collaborative projects play an important role in HASS learning. Reggio Emilia approach discovers inquiry and numerous paths of expressing ideas, and children are encouraged to share their knowledge of people and places creatively (Littledyke, 2014). Gardner's multiple intelligences theory detects interpersonal and intrapersonal intelligences that are supported by activities like role-play, interviewing, and personal stories (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Piaget's constructivist principle affirms experiential learning, such as community walks. These principles suggest that HASS is best learned through significant, practical, and innovative activities.

Resources, Materials, and Digital Technologies

HASS learning can also use maps, globes, photographs, and cultural artifacts (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Story books from various cultures educate children about diversity and the past. Loose materials, such as blocks and recycled materials, can be used to model landmarks or communities. Digital technologies, including interactive maps, virtual tours, and child-friendly websites, can support learning (ACARA, 2021). DVDs of historical events or cultural festivals can stimulate children's creative reactions through art, role-play, or stories. The combination of real objects, stories, and technology provides rich and interactive potential for children to discover and understand people, places, and cultures.

EYLF Outcome 4: Children observe and record environmental changes.

Children draw simple maps of the classroom or playground with symbols (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

EYLF Outcome 4: Children represent places and spaces.

Children re-create cultural events, such as Lunar New Year or NAIDOC Week, through songs, dance, and costumes (Stonehouse, 2012).

EYLF Outcome 2: Children respect and value diversity.

Integrated Curriculum (STEM/STEAM)

Importance in Facilitating Creativity

Curriculum models incorporated such as STEM, STEAM, STREAM, and STEMIE combine science, technology, engineering, and mathematics with subjects like the arts, reading, and inclusion (Zabawa, 2019). In early childhood, these models help children in problem-solving, critical thinking, and creativity through play and project-based hands-on activities. By integrating subjects, children learn how subjects connect. For example, they would design a bridge (engineering and mathematics), test materials (science), design it with digital technologies (technology), and decorate it (arts). The Early Years Learning Framework (EYLF) (AGDE, 2022) allows for this because it enables interdisciplinary and play-based learning. The experiences spark curiosity and set the children up for a lifetime of learning.

Creativity Theories and Perspectives

Vygotsky’s sociocultural theory shows that children learn best when working with others and receiving guidance. This is important in group projects. The Reggio Emilia approach encourages children to express ideas in many ways, such as art, building, and using technology (Littledyke, 2014). Gardner’s theory of multiple intelligences states that incorporating STEM into arts and literacy develops logical, spatial, language, and social skills (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). Piaget's constructivist theory improves experiential learning via explorations by hand. These theories suggest that incorporating the curriculum makes creativity better by mixing different skills, which encourages team collaboration and enabling children to solve daily problems in an entertaining and interactive manner.

Integrated Learning Experiences

Age Group: 0–2 years

Colored Water Play

Babies explore shallow tubs of safe colored water using cups, funnels, and spoons. They watch how the water flows, pour it into different containers, and see colors mix together. Educators use simple language like “pour,” “full,” and “empty” to introduce early math and science concepts.

EYLF Outcome 4: Children explore cause and effect and early numeracy concepts (Wilson, n.d.).

Soft Block Stacking

Infants are given soft and textured blocks to stack, knock down, and rebuild. They explore shapes, sizes, and how blocks fit together. Educators encourage exploration by modelling stacking and naming shapes.

EYLF Outcome 4: Children develop spatial awareness and problem-solving skills (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

Age Group: 2–3 years

Rolling Cars on Ramps

Children use cardboard ramps to roll toy cars and balls, testing which travels faster or further. They experiment with changing ramp heights and notice differences in movement. Educators ask guiding questions such as “Which one will go faster?” to encourage prediction.

EYLF Outcome 4: Children investigate speed, distance, and movement (Major, 2018).

Leaf Sorting

During outdoor play, children collect leaves of different shapes, sizes, and colors. They bring them inside and sort them into groups. Educators introduce math language such as “big,” “small,” and “same.”

EYLF Outcome 5: Children practice sorting, classifying, and comparing (Fleer & Raban, n.d.).

Age Group: 3–5 years

Build a Paper Tower

Children work in pairs to build the tallest tower they can using only paper and tape. They test different folding and rolling methods to make the paper stronger. Educators encourage problem-solving by asking how they can make it taller or more stable.

EYLF Outcome 4: Children design, test, and improve ideas (Zabawa, 2019).

Story Building

After listing picture books, children build a setting from the book with blocks, recycled materials, and art materials. They can add characters, props, and labels. Teachers ask them to discuss what they built and why they chose to do some things.

EYLF Outcome 5: Children connect literacy with design and art (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

Critical Reflection

Movement and Music

"Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes" Dance (2–3 years)

The "Head, Shoulders, Knees, and Toes" Dance was highly in demand. Children enjoyed the actions and song, picking up on this rapidly with both movement and singing. This helped them remember the order of body parts and improved listening and memory skills. The repetition supported language development, and the actions encouraged coordination and body awareness (Niland, 2015). Children also copied each other’s movements, showing peer learning and cooperation.

I would accelerate the tempo of the song from slow to fast to make it exciting and demanding. I would also incorporate simple props such as headbands or stickers to label body parts and keep children alert.

Homemade Instrument Band (3–5 years)

The Homemade Instrument Band was similarly interactive. Children enjoyed building instruments from recyclables and enjoyed exploring various sounds. Play together encouraged turn-taking, listening, and cooperation (Howard & Mayesky, 2022). It also opened a window for children to find rhythm, tempo, and loudness in a fun manner. I would tape the performance and then play it back to the children so that they can listen to themselves playing. I would provide them with some control in choosing their own song or making their own rhythms for purposes of ownership and creativity.

Language and Literacy

Puppet Storytelling (2–3 years)

The Puppet Storytelling activity worked, as children loved to bring stories to life through the puppets. They made animal sounds, acted out movements, and added their own ideas. Even the shy children contributed, showing how puppets can lead to confidence and communication (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

An improvement on this activity would be to provide a small puppet stage or backdrop. This would focus the play, structure the story, and enrich the experience for both the participating children and observers.

The Class Story Creation with Caterpillar Craft (3–5 years)

The Class Story Creation with Caterpillar Craft worked well. Children shared ideas for characters and settings, listened to each other, and worked together to make a complete story. They enjoyed seeing their ideas on paper and creating colorful caterpillar crafts using paper circles, glue, and decorations. This activity supported creativity, teamwork, and fine motor skills (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

An improvement would be to make the story in smaller steps. Children could first work in pairs or small groups, then join together. This would help them stay focused, take part actively, and make sure everyone’s ideas were included.

Science

Magnet Discovery (2–3 years)

The Magnet Discovery activity worked well because the children were curious and eager to test different objects. They enjoyed predicting whether an item would stick to the magnet and were excited to check their results. The activity enabled language acquisition, problem-solving, and fine motor skills (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

An improvement for this activity would be to provide more types of objects, such as natural materials such as leaves or stones and man-made objects such as keys or paper clips. This would give children a larger ability to compare and sort, increasing the task and encouraging further investigation.

Sink or Float Investigation (3–5 years)

The Sink or Float Investigation also worked well. The children were engaged in testing each object and recording their results using drawings and symbols. This supported their observation skills, sorting abilities, and early data recording.

An improvement for this activity would be the inclusion of a short group discussion before the experiment when children can make predictions and a second discussion after testing when outcomes are contrasted. This will require children to justify their thoughts, listen, and build a richer understanding of the concepts being dealt with.

Engineering

Soft Block Stacking (0–2 years)

Soft Block Stacking was successful because the babies were highly engaged in touching, stacking, and knocking over the foam blocks. They tested the cause and effect by seeing what happened when blocks were stacked higher or toppled over. The activity challenged their sensory growth, fine motor skills, and pre-concept of balance (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

Bridge Building Challenge (3–5 years)

The Bridge Building Challenge activity was also successful. The children were eager to use blocks, straws, and sticks to design bridges. They tested their creations with toy cars or animals and showed persistence by making adjustments when the bridges collapsed. This activity supported problem-solving, teamwork, and creative thinking (Zabawa, 2019).

Technologies

Photo Exploration (2–3 years)

The Photo Exploration activity was effective because the children enjoyed capturing photos with the child-safe camera. They chose objects and people to take photos of carefully, pressed the button gently, and displayed their photos proudly to others. This activity enhanced their observation skills, fine motor control, and communication (NAEYC, n.d.).

An improvement for this activity would be to include a theme challenge, such as "find something that is red" or "take a picture of a circle." This would help focus children's vision, scrutinize their environment more closely, and think about in a creative sense what to take a picture of.

Digital Story Creation (3–5 years)

The Digital Story Creation activity also worked well because the children enjoyed creating their own stories using images and voice recordings. They worked together to choose characters and events, recorded their voices, and listened to the finished stories. This activity encouraged imagination, teamwork, and a sense of pride in their work (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

An improvement for this activity would be to provide props or dress-up clothes so children could act out their stories before recording them. This would combine technology with dramatic play and give children more ways to share and develop their ideas.

Mathematics and Numeracy

Shape Hunt (2–3 years)

Shape Hunt activity was successful because children enjoyed hunting for shapes inside and outside the classroom. They were thrilled to recognize shapes they had discovered using the cards and learned to recognize circles, squares, and triangles. The activity promoted the development of observation skills, vocabulary, and early recognition of shapes (McDonald, 2019).

An improvement suggestion for this activity is the addition of more shapes, such as rectangles, ovals, and stars. This would give children more exposure to new shapes to learn and practice matching and identifying different shapes.

Pattern Making (3–5 years)

Pattern Making activity was successful as children enjoyed making and extending patterns with beads, blocks, and natural materials. A few of the children invented their own patterns, which encouraged creativity, sequencing, and problem-solving (Littledyke, 2014).

An alternative to this activity would be to give small tasks, i.e., "make a repeating pattern using three colors or shapes." This would test children further to anticipate and create more complex pattern-making abilities.

Humanities and social sciences

Family Photo Sharing (0–2 years)

The Family Photo Sharing activity worked well because the babies enjoyed looking at familiar faces in the photos. They listened with interest when educators named family members and described their roles. This supported early language skills, a sense of belonging, and social connection (Stonehouse, 2012). It also helped strengthen relationships between the child, educator, and family.

An improvement would be to invite families to share photos of special events or cultural celebrations. This would allow children to see and hear about their own traditions and help educators introduce and discuss different cultures within the group.

Map-Making (3–5 years)

The Map-Making activity worked because kids enjoyed drawing playground maps and dropping symbols for swings, sandpits, and other features. This supported spatial awareness, creativity, and problem-solving (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

An improvement for this activity would be to take children on a short “mapping walk” before they start drawing. This would help them notice more details in the environment and include them in their maps.

Integrated curriculum (STEM, STEAM, STREAM & STEMIE)

Build a Paper Tower (2–3 years)

The Build a Paper Tower activity succeeded due to children enjoying folding and building paper into tall towers. They tried out how to keep the paper stable and improvised when the tower collapsed. This enhanced problem-solving, hand–eye coordination, and early engineering capabilities in them (Fleer & Raban, n.d.).

One modification of this exercise is to give children a range of paper material, for example, cardboard, newspaper, and tissue paper. This would enable them to see how each of these different materials affects the strength and stability of the tower and think about more and attempt more.

Rolling Cars on Ramps (3–5 years)

Rolling Cars on Ramps was a success since kids loved creating ramps using cardboard and tubes, rolling the cars, and watching how far they travel. They learned that changing the height or slope of the ramp makes the cars travel at different speeds. This embedded scientific thinking, prediction, and collaboration (Howard & Mayesky, 2022).

An improvement for this activity would be to provide a measuring tape so that children are able to measure and track the distance traveled by the cars. This would increase early numeracy and enable them to compare, which will result in discussion and critical thinking.

Full Introduction

Early childhood teachers have a very important role in supporting children’s creativity. Teachers can create rich learning environments where children feel safe to explore and take risks. They can provide open-ended materials, such as paints, blocks, clay, fabrics, puppets, musical instruments, and digital tools. These resources allow children to choose how they want to create and what they want to express. Teachers can also ask open-ended questions like “What do you think will happen next?” or “Can you show me another way to do that?” This helps children think deeply and extend their ideas.

The Early Years Learning Framework (EYLF) highlights how creativity supports many learning outcomes. Outcome 4 focuses on children being confident and involved learners. When children are creative, they develop curiosity, problem-solving skills, and the confidence to try new things. Outcome 5 focuses on communication. Creative activities help children share their ideas through drawing, writing, storytelling, drama, movement, and music (DEEWR, 2009). Creativity also supports Outcome 2, where children learn to connect with others and contribute to their world. Group projects, like murals, plays, or musical performances, help children work together and learn from one another.

There are many ways teachers can support creativity across different curriculum areas. In art, children use colours, shapes, and textures to show their ideas and feelings. In drama and puppetry, they take on different roles and tell stories. Through movement and music, children explore rhythm, sound, and expression. In language and literacy, they create their own stories, poems, or books.

Creativity is also important in mathematics and science. When children build with blocks or explore patterns, they are learning to think creatively. In technologies and engineering, children design, plan, and make things to solve problems. In humanities and social sciences, creativity helps children understand people, places, and cultures. STEM,STEAM, STREAM, and STEMIE approaches bring all these areas together so children can learn through projects that link science, technology, engineering, the arts, and maths.

This website will share how teachers can support creativity across all these curriculum areas. Each page will provide simple explanations, learning theories, resources, and clear examples of activities for different age groups. The aim is to help teachers plan creative, fun, and meaningful learning that helps children grow as confident, capable, and imaginative learners.
